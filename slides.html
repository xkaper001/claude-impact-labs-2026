The outcome today

A found person at one center stays invisible to a family at another. The match often already exists in the data — and nobody can see it.

86% reunification sounds fine until you see it's despite no cross-center search — driven by PA announcements and luck. The 350 are exactly the cases a cross-center matcher targets.

The failure we fix

Two centers. One person. Zero connection.

⟶ CENTER A · "Found"

A confused elderly man arrives at the Ramkund center. No ID, can't recall his name. A volunteer files a record here.

⟵ CENTER B · "Searching"

3 km away his family describes him at a different center. They never see the record from Center A.

No cross-search.  Hours pass. On a snan day, hours are dangerous.

This is the single highest-impact gap, straight from the dataset brief. Everything we build serves closing it.

Why naive matching fails

The data is incomplete by nature

A matcher built for clean records is useless here.

  • 15% have no name — must never be penalised
  • 20% have no phone — the at-risk group is phoneless
  • 8% are the same person filed across centers

So we score on what families do know — origin state, age, clothing — and treat missing fields honestly, never as a mismatch.

Incomplete data is the design constraint, not an edge case. Our engine excludes unassessable fields with a note instead of guessing or penalising.

Where the impact lands

Compressing the dangerous tail

470 cases took over 6 hours to resolve.

That long tail is overwhelmingly the cross-center cases — found here, searched there. Surfacing the match in <0.5s is where KumbhRakshak turns 6+ hours into a phone call to the right center.

Average resolution is 4h, but the tail (>6h) is the human cost — and exactly the cross-center pattern. We don't claim to teleport people; we remove the "who and where" guesswork.

Responsible & deployable by design

Guardrails are built in

It will NEVER

  • Confirm a match — only "possible," volunteer verifies
  • Ask for Aadhaar / PAN / any government ID
  • Store or repeat a reporter's mobile number
  • Decide child custody — escalate to police

It ALWAYS

  • Auto-escalates: child, health condition, case > 6h
  • Flags ≥3 reports of one person (trafficking, sensitively)
  • Runs offline: PouchDB ⇄ CouchDB peer sync
  • Stays deterministic & parseable under spike
Privacy by design + human-in-the-loop is exactly the rubric's "responsible data handling." The NEVER block is a hard constraint in the prompt and the engine.
